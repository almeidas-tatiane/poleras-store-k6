## Executive Summary

The products-service catalog was stress tested at 100→2,000 VUs (2 min/stage) after implementing Node.js cluster mode (4 workers) and Redis TTL extension (300s). The service reached 2,000 VUs with **zero errors and zero interrupted iterations**, a complete reversal from Run 1 (~750 VU crash) and Run 2 (~950 VU crash). The P95 latency SLA (100ms) was maintained up to **~1,600 VUs** and only breached gracefully at peak — the primary fix required for Black Friday (2,500 VU target) is increasing cluster workers from 4 to 8.

## Findings

### HIGH — P95 Latency SLA Breach Above ~1,600 VUs

**Observed:**
P95 for `/api/categories` and `/api/products` first exceeded the 100ms SLA at ~1,600 VUs (Prometheus: 162ms and 167ms respectively at t+13m). At 2,000 VUs (Stage 6 peak), all three endpoints reached 335–342ms P95. The k6 global P95 was 778ms (heavily weighted by the 2,000 VU phase). Median (P50) remained 25ms throughout, indicating the majority of requests stayed fast.

**Root cause hypothesis:**
Node.js event loop saturation at > 1,600 VUs. Each of the 4 cluster workers handles ~400 VUs at peak Stage 6. With 3 async operations per VU iteration (Redis GET × 3 + optional DB query), the per-worker event loop queue grows beyond real-time processing capacity, causing response callbacks to stack. This matches Pattern 8 (Event Loop / Async Saturation).

**Supporting evidence:**
- Prometheus instant P95 at t+11m32s (1,811 VUs) showed 24.7–24.9ms — within SLA. The degradation is non-linear (exponential above ~1,400 VUs), consistent with event loop queue buildup.
- P50 = 25ms throughout the test even while P95 = 778ms: a wide spread (p95 >> p50) is the signature of event loop saturation, not CPU or DB saturation.
- DB connections peak: 5/25 per worker (20% of pool) — pool is NOT exhausted; the bottleneck is not the DB layer.

**Impact:**
At 1,600 VUs, 1 in 20 users of the catalog experiences response times > 162ms. At 2,000 VUs, 1 in 20 users waits > 335ms. Both exceed the 100ms SLA. No users receive errors.

**Recommended action:**
Increase `NODE_CLUSTER_WORKERS` from 4 to 8 in `docker-compose.yml`. Each worker currently saturates at ~400 VUs; 8 workers would push the saturation point to ~3,200 VUs — 28% above the Black Friday 2,500 VU target. Adjust `DB_POOL_MAX` proportionally: 13 per worker (8 × 13 = 104 total ≈ current 100).

**Owner:** Backend Engineering
**Effort estimate:** Low (env variable change + redeploy)
**Retest required:** Yes

### MEDIUM — 4 DB Connection Timeout Errors at 2,000 VU Peak

**Observed:**
Loki shows 4 error log entries at 22:35:45–46 UTC (t≈12m46s, during Stage 7 ramp-down from 2,000 VUs):
- `"Database query error"` → `"error":"Connection terminated due to connection timeout"` on `table: categories`
- `"HTTP request failed"` → `statusCode: 500`, `duration: ~5,500–6,020ms`, `path: "/api/categories"`

k6 confirmed: 4 out of 283,954 requests failed (0.00% error rate — within the 0.5% SLA).

**Root cause hypothesis (Tempo trace confirmed):**
Trace `156e2ac32bc9708d619adbccb0b955f5` reveals the failure chain:
1. `redis-GET catalog:categories`: **464ms** (normally <1ms). Under 2,000 VU load, Redis response time degraded — indicating Redis itself is under memory or network pressure with simultaneous connections from 4 workers.
2. After slow Redis response, the system fell through to DB. `tcp.connect` to `products-db:5432`: **1,068ms** — a new TCP connection took over 1 second to establish, exceeding the DB connection timeout.
3. DNS resolution for `products-db`: **612ms** (overlapping with TCP connect) — under load, even DNS lookups experience delays.

**Supporting evidence:**
- All 4 errors affect only `/api/categories` — the endpoint with the broadest query (full table scan) and the highest chance of a synchronized TTL expiry (cache stampede at 300s TTL boundary).
- DB pool peak: 5 active connections — pool was not exhausted. The failure was connection establishment timeout, not pool exhaustion.
- traceId in Loki matches Tempo: `156e2ac32bc9708d619adbccb0b955f5` (confirmed).

**Grafana evidence:**
- Loki errors: `{container="products-service"} |= "error"` — window: 2026-06-16T22:23:00Z to 22:38:00Z
- Tempo trace: traceId `156e2ac32bc9708d619adbccb0b955f5` — span `redis-GET` 464ms → `tcp.connect` 1,068ms
- Deeplink: `http://localhost:3000/explore?orgId=1&datasource=tempo&left={"queries":[{"query":"156e2ac32bc9708d619adbccb0b955f5","queryType":"traceql"}]}`

**Impact:**
4 errors in 283,954 requests = 0.0014% error rate. Well within the 0.5% SLA. At Black Friday scale (2,500 VUs with current 4 workers), error rate would increase significantly due to more frequent event loop congestion causing Redis slowdowns and DB timeouts.

**Recommended action:**
Two parallel fixes:
1. Increase workers to 8 (primary fix — reduces per-worker concurrency, prevents Redis/TCP congestion)
2. Add TTL jitter to Redis cache keys (e.g., TTL = 300s ± 30s) to prevent synchronized cache expiry (thundering herd) across all 4 workers simultaneously hitting the DB.

**Owner:** Backend Engineering
**Effort estimate:** Low
**Retest required:** Yes

### INFORMATIONAL — `/api/products/:slug` More Resilient Than List Endpoints

**Observed:**
At 1,600–1,800 VUs, `/api/products/:slug` P95 held at 54–55ms while `/api/categories` and `/api/products` (list) breached 162–173ms. All three converged at 335ms at 2,000 VUs.

**Root cause hypothesis:**
Slug lookups access a specific product by unique key — higher cache locality (same products likely re-requested by VUs), smaller result set, and simpler query. List endpoints return N records and have lower cache hit specificity at concurrent load.

**Impact:** Informational — no action required. Confirms caching is working correctly for high-traffic single-item lookups.

**Recommended action:** No action required.

## Test Conditions and Limitations

- **Environment:** Local Docker on development machine. Results are relative baselines — absolute numbers will differ in production (different CPU, network, memory).
- **Dataset:** k6 users dataset; products catalog is the full Docker seed (no data volume constraints tested).
- **Known limitations:** Redis is a single shared instance serving all 5 microservices. Products-service Redis latency will be higher in a full e2e test. CPU/memory metrics not available (no cAdvisor).
- **What this test proves:** The products-service can sustain 0.00% error rate up to 2,000 VUs and maintains P95 < 100ms up to ~1,600 VUs with cluster mode. The 4-worker configuration is insufficient for Black Friday at 2,500 VUs — 8 workers are required.
- **What this test does not prove:** Behavior under sustained load (soak), simultaneous load from all 5 services (e2e), or production infrastructure capacity.

## What We Tested

We simulated up to 2,000 customers simultaneously browsing the Poleras Store catalog — viewing product categories, product listings, and individual product pages. This represents the expected shopping behavior during the Black Friday event, where 2,500 concurrent shoppers are anticipated. The test ran for 14 minutes, progressively increasing from 100 to 2,000 simultaneous users to find the exact point where the system begins to struggle.

---

## Is It Ready?

**Verdict:** Not ready — one action required before Black Friday

The catalog service can now handle nearly double the traffic it could after the first round of improvements — a significant engineering achievement. However, it still falls short of the 2,500-customer Black Friday target by a meaningful margin. One targeted change (described below) is expected to push capacity 28% above the target, and is estimated as a low-effort fix.

---

## Risks

| Risk | Business impact | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Catalog browsing slows for 1 in 20 shoppers above ~1,600 simultaneous users | During Black Friday peak, shoppers experience slow product pages — increasing cart abandonment and reducing conversion | Must fix before launch |
| 4 individual checkout-flow requests returned errors at peak 2,000-user load | Extremely rare under current settings (~1 in 70,000 requests), but will increase significantly if all 5 services run simultaneously under full Black Friday load | Fix before launch |
| Shared infrastructure (Redis cache) degraded under single-service 2,000-user load | When all 5 services run simultaneously during Black Friday, Redis may show worse degradation than seen in this test | Monitor — run full e2e stress test |

---

## What Happens If We Launch Now

If Black Friday proceeds with the current setup, the catalog service will perform well up to approximately 1,600 simultaneous shoppers. Beyond that threshold — which the 2,500-user Black Friday target exceeds by 56% — approximately 1 in 20 customers will experience noticeably slower product pages (2–3× the normal response time). No customers will see error pages; shopping carts and payments will be unaffected. The experience degrades gradually rather than failing suddenly, which limits the damage — but slow pages directly reduce the likelihood that browsing customers complete a purchase.

---

## What Needs to Happen Before Launch

- **Server capacity must be increased** — The catalog service needs to be configured to use more processing capacity on the same machines. This is a configuration-only change (no code required) and is estimated to take less than a day to implement and verify. Without it, peak Black Friday traffic will cause slower browsing experiences for a significant portion of shoppers.

---

## What Can Wait

- **Redis cache tuning** — A minor improvement to how cache entries expire (staggering expirations to avoid simultaneous database requests) would eliminate the 4 isolated errors seen at peak load. Given the extremely low occurrence rate, this is a low-urgency improvement.
- **Full e2e stress test** — A test simulating all 5 services simultaneously under Black Friday load should be run once the catalog capacity fix is verified. This will reveal any shared infrastructure bottlenecks (cache, database) not visible in single-service testing.

---

## Decision Required

**Question:** Should the catalog service proceed to the next phase of Black Friday readiness testing, or should the capacity fix be applied and retested first?

**Options:**

| Option | Benefit | Risk |

**Recommendation:** Apply the capacity fix first. The change is low-effort and low-risk (configuration only). Running the full-stack test with a known bottleneck wastes time and produces misleading results.
