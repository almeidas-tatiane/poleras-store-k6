## Executive Summary

A stress test on the products catalog service (products-service) was executed with a staged ramp from 100 to 2,000 VUs across 14 minutes. The service **breached its P95 SLA of 100ms at approximately 750 VUs** (Stage 4: 800 VUs ramp), with latency rising exponentially from 41ms at 700 VUs to 359ms at 750 VUs and ultimately 9,087ms at 2,000 VUs. The root cause is PostgreSQL connection pool exhaustion — the pool is capped at 25 connections while each VU iteration executes 3 sequential DB read queries, saturating the pool at ~750 VUs. Critically, the service degraded **gracefully with zero errors** throughout the entire test (0.00% http_req_failed, 100% checks passed, 0 Loki entries) — it queues rather than crashes. The products-service handles **30% of the 2,500 VU Black Friday target** before SLA degradation begins.

## Findings

### 🔴 CRITICAL — PostgreSQL Connection Pool Exhausted at ~750 VUs

**Observed:** P95 jumped 756% in a single 30-second window (41.92ms → 359.32ms) as VUs crossed ~750 during Stage 4. Latency then grew exponentially with each additional VU, reaching 9,087ms at 2,000 VUs. Zero errors were generated throughout — the service queued all requests without dropping a single one.

**Root cause:** The products-service PostgreSQL connection pool is capped at **25 connections** (inherited service default). Each VU iteration executes **3 sequential DB read queries** (categories → product list → product detail). At ~750 VUs with ~5s average think time per iteration, the effective request concurrency reaches ~250 simultaneous DB operations. With only 25 connections available, requests queue at the pool level waiting for a connection to free. Once the queue grows, latency cascades exponentially — consistent with **M/G/1 queuing theory past saturation**: each additional VU adds to the queue without increasing throughput.

**Supporting evidence:**
- Latency jump was **instantaneous** (not gradual) at ~750 VUs → pool saturation threshold, not CPU ramp-up
- Exponential latency growth pattern (41ms → 359ms → 785ms → 1,083ms → 2,217ms) matching queuing buildup
- **0% error rate throughout** — service never rejected requests, only slowed → proper pool backpressure
- **100% checks passed** — all 3 endpoints returned valid data even at 9,087ms P95
- **0 Loki entries** (173,262 lines scanned) — no exceptions, no pool timeout logs, no DB errors
- `http_req_failed = 0.00%` — k6 never received a failed HTTP response from any of the 173,146 requests
- Throughput: 68.28 iter/s (~204.8 req/s) — service continued processing without dropping load

**Comparison with auth bottleneck:**
Unlike auth (bcrypt CPU saturation — event loop blocked), the products bottleneck is **I/O wait** (DB connection queuing). Auth has a CPU ceiling where adding more cores helps; products has a connection ceiling where increasing the pool size directly raises the breaking point.

**Recommended actions (ranked):**

| # | Action | Effort | Expected gain |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Increase PostgreSQL pool size: 25 → 100 connections | Low | ~4× breaking point increase (~3,000 VUs) |
| 2 | Add Redis read cache for catalog endpoints (TTL: 30–60s) | Medium | Eliminates DB queries for cache hits; 80%+ pool reduction |
| 3 | Add PgBouncer connection pooler between app and PostgreSQL | Medium | Multiplexes many app connections into few DB connections |
| 4 | Optimize queries: add indexes on `slug`, `category_id` columns | Low | Reduces per-query time → frees connections faster |
| 5 | Horizontal scaling — 2+ products-service instances | Low | Linear pool scaling; immediate relief |

**Owner:** Backend Engineering (products-service)
**Retest required:** Yes — confirm breaking point ≥ 1,500 VUs after pool increase

## What We Tested

We simulated an increasing number of customers browsing the Poleras Store product catalog simultaneously — starting from 100 customers and stepping up to 2,000 — to find the point where the catalog starts responding too slowly. The product catalog is the first service every shopper uses: before adding anything to a cart or making a purchase, they browse categories and product listings. Black Friday requires the platform to serve 2,500 customers simultaneously at acceptable speed.

---

## Is It Ready?

**Verdict: NOT READY — The catalog service handles approximately 30% of Black Friday demand before slowing down**

The catalog service begins to slow beyond acceptable thresholds at around 750 simultaneous shoppers — 30% of the Black Friday target. Unlike the login service which started returning errors under stress, the catalog never failed: every customer eventually received their product listings, just with increasing wait times. At 2,000 customers simultaneously, catalog pages were taking up to 9 seconds to load. The good news is the fix is straightforward and low-risk.

---

## Risks

| Risk | Business impact | Urgency |
|---|---|---|
| Catalog slows at 750 simultaneous shoppers | Customers browsing products see increasing delays before they can add to cart — directly reducing the time available for purchasing during the event | **Must fix before Black Friday** |
| 3.3× capacity gap to Black Friday target | At 2,500 shoppers, catalog pages could take 9+ seconds to load — most customers will abandon before adding to cart | **Must fix before Black Friday** |
| Login service remains the weakest link | Authentication (required before any purchase) breaks even earlier than the catalog — the catalog fix is not sufficient on its own | Test and fix urgently |
| Recovery takes 2–3 minutes after peak | After a traffic spike, catalog pages remain slow for 2–3 minutes while the system recovers — impacting the post-peak shopping window | Monitor and plan for |

---

## What Happens If We Launch Now

Under Black Friday conditions, the product catalog will begin slowing down within the first minute of the event as customer numbers rise. Shoppers who arrive when the site is at peak traffic will experience catalog pages taking several seconds to load. Many will abandon before finding products they want to buy. Since customers cannot add items to their cart without first seeing them in the catalog, a slow catalog directly reduces purchase conversion during the busiest hours of the event.

The catalog does not crash — it keeps working slowly — but a slow catalog is effectively invisible to the customer who clicks "Back" after 3 seconds.

---

## What Needs to Happen Before Launch

- **Expand the database connection pool** — the catalog is currently limited to 25 simultaneous database queries, which becomes a bottleneck at ~750 customers. Increasing this limit to 100 is a configuration change (not a code change) and is expected to raise the capacity to ~3,000 customers — above the Black Friday target. This is the highest-priority, lowest-effort fix.
- **Add a catalog cache** — product listings and categories rarely change during a sale. Storing them in a fast memory cache means most customer requests never hit the database at all, dramatically reducing load. This is the highest-value fix for sustained peak capacity.
- **Complete stress tests on the remaining 3 services** — the cart, order, and payment services have not yet been tested under stress. Any of them may have their own capacity limits that would block customers from completing purchases.
- **Re-verify after fixes** — confirm the catalog handles at least 1,500–2,000 customers simultaneously with acceptable speed before Black Friday.

---

## What We Can Defer

- Detailed recovery time measurement — the catalog recovers within a few minutes after load drops; this is acceptable for operational planning but does not change the go/no-go decision
- Adding detailed monitoring metrics to the catalog service — useful but not blocking launch

---

## Decision Required

**Question:** Can Poleras Store proceed with Black Friday as currently planned?

| Option | Benefit | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| **Expand DB pool + add cache, then retest (recommended)** | Directly addresses the bottleneck; configuration change is fast and low-risk | Requires 1–3 days of engineering and testing |
| **Launch with only the DB pool fix (no cache)** | Faster to implement | Estimated 3,000 VU capacity — meets target but with no safety margin for traffic spikes above forecast |
| **Launch as-is** | No engineering time required | Near-certain catalog slowdowns at peak; conversion impact during the event |

**Recommendation:** Implement the database pool expansion and add Redis catalog caching, then retest. Both fixes combined are expected to push catalog capacity well above the Black Friday target. Do not launch Black Friday without this validation.

---

**Root cause of the most critical problem:** PostgreSQL connection pool capped at 25 simultaneous database connections. At ~750 customers browsing simultaneously, incoming requests queue waiting for a database connection — causing latency to grow exponentially while the service remains stable (zero errors).

**Fix first: Increase the connection pool from 25 to 100 connections** — a configuration change in the products-service environment settings. Expected outcome: breaking point moves from ~750 VUs to ~3,000 VUs, exceeding the Black Friday target of 2,500 VUs.
